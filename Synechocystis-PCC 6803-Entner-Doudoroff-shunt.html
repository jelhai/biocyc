Synechocystis sp. PCC 6803 PCC Pathway: Entner-Doudoroff shunt


If an enzyme name is shown in bold, there is experimental evidence for this enzymatic activity.

BioCyc ID: ENTNER-DOUDOROFF-PWY

Superclasses: Generation of Precursor Metabolites and Energy → Entner-Doudoroff Pathways

Summary:
The Entner-Doudoroff shunt META:ENTNER-DOUDOROFF-PWY META/ENTNER-DOUDOROFF-PWY [How to make this link to the Metacyc page?] takes D-gluconate 6-phosphate produced by the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway and converts it to pyruvate and D-glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate (which enters into glycolysis or other pathways). It consists of two reactions: (1) 6-phosphogluconate dehydratase (encoded by edd, slr0452) and (2) KDPG aldolase (encoded by eda, sll0107). When combined with the first step of the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway and lower glycolysis ( D-glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to pyruvate), the pathway produces one fewer ATP per hexose than glycolysis. Nonetheless, it is more important in Synechocystis PCC 6803 than glycolysis in physiological contexts where the two have been compared.

The Entner-Doudoroff pathway enables Synechocystis PCC 6803 to grow most efficiently on light in the presence of glucose (photomixotrophic growth), as mutation of sll0107 encoding KDPG aldolase reduces growth under mixotrophic conditions, while no growth impairment results from the mutation of genes ( pfk1 and pfk2) encoding phosphofructokinase, central to glycolysis. Others, using 13C tracer experiments, have disputed this conclusion, providing evidence that the growth dependence on the Entner-Doudoroff pathway results from transient light limitation produced by the growth conditions of Chen et al (2016) [Schulze22][Makowka20]. The Entner-Doudoroff pathway is sufficient for photomixotrophic growth, even with the mutation of genes essential to glycolysis ( pfk1 and pfk2) and the oxidative pentose phosphate pathway ( gnd) beyond the point required by the Entner-Doudoroff pathway [Chen16].

A critical role for the Entner-Doudoroff pathway may be in the utilization of carbon stored in glycogen, which accumulates, for example, during nitrogen deprivation. Removal of a nitrogen source from the growth medium causes Synechocystis PCC 6803 to cannibalize the photosynthetic apparatus for amino acids, leading to loss of green pigment, a process called chlorosis [Forchhammer19]. Recovery from this state by the addition of a nitrogen source depends on D-glucose 6-phosphate resulting from glycogen breakdown, and its subsequent metabolism. Use of D-glucose 6-phosphate and recovery from chlorosis is unaffected by the loss of glycolysis through mutation of phosphofructokinase, but it is nearly abolished by the loss of the Entner-Doudoroff pathway through mutation of KDPG aldolase [Doello18]. Similarly, utilization of glycogen stores accumulated under high light is severely impaired when the Entner-Doudoroff pathway is absent by mutation [Lucius21]. Glycogen stores are increased and consumed through light/dark cycles during photoautotrophic growth, and under these conditions, growth is markedly affected by the loss of the Entner-Doudoroff pathway but not by the loss of glycolysis [Chen16] [Schulze22].

The Entner-Doudoroff pathway may play an important role in replenishing intermediates of the Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle. This is indicated by the relative failure of a mutant lacking the pathway to activate the Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle of dark-adapted cells upon illumination [Makowka20].

The genomes of almost all cyanobacteria have the potential of encoding the two enzymes of the Entner-Doudoroff pathway (with strains of Microcystis being notable exceptions). In contrast, many cyanobacteria lack genes encoding phosphofructokinase and therefore the ability to break down glucose through glycolysis [Chen16].
